Gym Training, Progress Tracking, and a Nod to Mike Mentzer

This page is built from a blank HTML file with a custom stylesheet (no Bootstrap). It focuses on training structure, tracking progress, and the idea that effort and recovery matter as much as the workout itself.

Mike Mentzer (Short, Useful Summary)

Mike Mentzer popularized high-intensity training (HIT): fewer total working sets, very high effort, and a serious focus on recovery. The core point is simple: if you train hard enough to create a strong stimulus, you may not need endless volume to make progress. Whether you follow HIT strictly or not, Mentzer’s emphasis on training with purpose (and not “junk volume”) is a helpful filter for building an efficient routine.

My takeaway: train with intent, track your performance, and let recovery support the next session.

Weekly Structure (Nested List Requirement)

I keep a simple schedule so it’s easy to be consistent. Here’s an ordered plan with an embedded unordered list.

  1. Strength (3 days)
    • Push: bench/incline + overhead press + triceps
    • Pull: row/pulldown + rear delts + biceps
    • Legs: squat pattern + hinge pattern + calves
  2. Conditioning (2 days)
    • Zone 2: 20–40 minutes
    • Intervals: short and controlled
  3. Mobility (daily)
    • Hips, shoulders, ankles (5–10 minutes)

The goal is clarity: if you can’t explain your plan in one minute, it’s probably too complicated.
